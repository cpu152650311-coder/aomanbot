Commercial buyers are no longer asking whether service robots belong in operations—they are deciding *where* to place the first standardized deployments so that labor gaps, service promises, and capital limits line up. The latest cycle of industry data shows professional-use machines clustering around logistics-style transport, maturing cleaning fleets, and hospitality programs that are being refined through clearer task definitions rather than sheer pilot volume. That sequence matters for operators who run mixed environments such as hotels, campuses, and light-industrial sites where the same brand must present a calm guest experience while backstage workflows stay predictable.

According to the International Federation of Robotics (IFR), in its **World Robotics 2025 – Service Robots** release, worldwide sales of new professional service robots reached roughly **199,000 units in 2024**, about **nine percent** above the prior year, illustrating how organizations continue to place hard orders even when macro budgets tighten. Takayuki Ito, President of the IFR, ties part of that momentum to procurement flexibility, noting in the federation’s October 2025 briefing that “more-and-more companies are deciding to enter into subscription or rental agreements rather than purchasing robots outright” and highlighting Robot-as-a-Service, where the tracked fleet expanded about **31 percent** to **more than 24,500** subscribed units, with rental-style channels in logistics growing about **42 percent** in 2024. A tangible pattern in mixed-use properties is therefore a daytime emphasis on guest-visible assistance and light goods movement, paired with overnight runs that concentrate on cleaning and restocking so staff overlap is minimized at peak service hours.

## Indoor transport still leads the professional tally

The weight of enterprise demand continues to sit on autonomous transport of goods and materials inside controlled facilities. IFR classifies the leading professional segment as **transportation and logistics**, recording about **102,900 units sold in 2024**, an increase near **fourteen percent**, which means more than half of professional service robot shipments were tied to moving totes, carts, or pallets in settings that range from factory aisles to back-of-house corridors in large venues. The same IFR release quotes President **Takayuki Ito** observing that **“there is strong demand for service robots in a number of different application areas,”** a remark that squares with the federation’s count of roughly **333** identified suppliers focused on logistics-type mobile robots—evidence of both competition and sustained order flow rather than a single-season spike. Boston Consulting Group, in its client research on warehouse network automation (*Amplify Your Warehouse Automation ROI*), reports that leading consumer-facing operators pursuing disciplined automation programs have documented on the order of **20 to 50 percent** gains in service levels alongside **25 to 50 percent** reductions in fulfillment costs, outcomes that help explain why mobility budgets are prioritized before many front-of-house experiments. In practice, a regional retailer might deploy mobile platforms to shuttle cases from receiving to picking while a sister property in hospitality uses structurally similar carriers—often with different payload skids—for linens and amenity crates along service elevators, allowing a single OEM relationship to cover two capex committees.

## Hospitality: fewer units, sharper use cases

Hospitality remained the second-largest application cluster in IFR’s 2024 count, with more than **42,000 robots** sold, yet the federation also records a **decline near eleven percent** that signals a market cooling from early novelty purchases toward sustained workflows. Industry reporting on Middle East hotel investment describes operators leaning on robotics as wage structures and guest expectations diverge; *Travel Daily Media* has profiled properties including **Address Downtown Dubai**, where technology-assisted concierge-style services reduce peak-period front-desk load, and **W Doha**, which worked with robotics specialists on lobby service concepts—examples of flagship assets treating robots as branded guest touchpoints while engineering teams still insist on concrete run-hours and escalation paths. Separately, academic fieldwork on **Hotel Hanaholmen** in Finland documents deployment of the mobile assistant **Kalle** for meal and amenity deliveries; the case study cautions that low utilization can limit physical workload savings even when sentiment toward the robot stays positive, a reminder that route density and hand-off design matter as much as hardware polish.

## Professional cleaning robots compound labor strategy

Professional cleaning jumped about **34 percent** to more than **25,000 units** sold in 2024, according to IFR’s service-robot publication, with floor care as the dominant task—precisely the category that spans airports, retail concourses, clinical corridors, and hotel ballrooms. Dr. Werner Kraus, Chair of the IFR Service Robot Committee, connects laboratory and clinical pressure to broader service trends in the same IFR release, stating that “in times of labor scarcity, service robots help to reduce manual labor” while demand for medical services rises with an aging population; facilities without laboratories still borrow the lesson that overnight repeatable routes beat understaffed swing shifts. Operators often pair scrubbers with delivery-style platforms so that one vendor footprint handles both visible cleanliness and back-of-house cart runs, aligning capital reviews under a single service-level objective rather than competing pilot KPIs.

## Financing models meet scale-up realities

Capital friction—not sensor novelty—is frequently the constraint service teams cite when a pilot stalls, which makes the IFR’s RaaS statistics more than a footnote. When Ito highlights subscription growth, he is describing a shift in governance: facilities managers can expense predictable monthly performance instead of lobbying for multi-year depreciation slots reserved for building systems. Gartner research cited in BCG’s warehouse automation analysis points toward **three-quarters** of large enterprises adopting intralogistics smart robots by **2026**, reinforcing why logistics-heavy portfolios receive board attention before discretionary lobby experiments; hospitality and cleaning buyers then adapt the same milestone reviews—pilot, hard ROI, network template—to guest-facing devices once mobility budgets normalize maintenance data. ## Humanoid and “multi-purpose” narratives versus serial production

IFR’s service-robot presentation materials explicitly temper expectations for humanoids: widespread mass deployment has not arrived, yet several manufacturers maintain small-series production and long-running R&D lines while newcomers remain at demonstrator scale, according to the federation’s 2025 market briefing. That framing mirrors what facility executives already observe—bipedal platforms draw media attention, but rolling platforms still absorb most verified operating hours in 2026 roadmaps. Buyers should therefore require human-height or anthropomorphic systems to clear the same workload evidence as any other AMR: mean time between assists, changeover steps when crowds thicken, and integration with existing property-management alarms. Where a humanoid or dual-arm station genuinely consolidates greeting, wayfinding, and light delivery without multiplying vendor risk, it earns budget; otherwise, specialized cleaners and cart-style robots continue to shoulder the measurable share of professional installations that IFR tracks.

## Outlook

The next wave of commercial robotics will be defined less by headline prototypes and more by repeatable routes—cleaning cycles that supervisors can audit, logistics loops that finance can compare across sites, and hospitality tasks where guest empathy and throughput share the same dashboard. IFR’s tallies make the priority order explicit: mobility for materiel, hygiene automation with steep year-on-year lift, then hospitality formats undergoing KPI discipline. Peripheral yet fast-growing clusters—search, rescue, and security applications at roughly **3,128 units** and near **nineteen percent growth** in IFR’s 2024 sample—also show where estates may fold patrol into the same command center that schedules scrubbers and carts. BCG’s warehouse economics and field cases such as Kalle at Hanaholmen show that the gap between demo and duty is operational, not mythical.
